The Tiranga — India’s National Flag

A concise guide to the flag’s origins, evolution, symbolism and how it became the emblem of modern India.

Designed by Pingali Venkayya. Officially adopted by the Constituent Assembly on 22 July 1947 (pre-independence). Below you can read the full journey of how the Tiranga evolved and what each part means.

Quick facts

  • Adopted: 22 July 1947
  • Designer: Pingali Venkayya
  • Proportions: 3:2
  • Central motif: Ashoka Chakra (24 spokes)

Complete history & evolution of the Tiranga

From early nationalist banners to the official tricolour — the flag’s story is the story of India’s freedom movement and nation building.

Early flags (1906–1920s)
Regional nationalist groups used tricolours and banners — for example a Calcutta flag of 1906 with green, yellow and red strips. These flags were symbolic expressions of local identity and anti-colonial sentiment.
The Swaraj Flag & the Charkha (1921)
Mahatma Gandhi proposed a flag featuring the spinning wheel (charkha) symbolizing self-reliance and Swadeshi. The 1921 design (by Pingali Venkayya's work and Congress deliberations) had saffron, white and green with a charkha in the middle.
Final design (1947)
After independence the Constituent Assembly chose a version that replaced Gandhi’s charkha with the Ashoka Chakra — representing the wheel of law — to emphasize India’s constitutional republic identity. Adopted: 22 July 1947.
Why the Ashoka Chakra?
The Ashoka Chakra is taken from the Lion Capital of Ashoka (Sarnath). It represents dharma (law/righteousness) and progress; the 24 spokes are said to represent 24 virtues or the 24 hours of the day — a constant movement of progress.
Color meanings (officially interpreted)
Saffron: courage and sacrifice. White: truth, peace and light. Green: faith, fertility and life.
Flag code & legal rules
Use of the flag is governed by the Flag Code of India (2002) and later amendments: proper display, handling, and prohibitions against misuse. The code standardizes flag proportions (3:2), colour shades and how the flag must be shown on vehicles, buildings and clothing.

Designer & important dates

Designer: Pingali Venkayya (an Indian freedom fighter and agriculturist who proposed designs during the Congress sessions).
Adopted by Constituent Assembly: 22 July 1947.
First time flown officially: On 15 August 1947 (Independence Day) it was ceremonially hoisted across India as the national flag of the independent nation.

Note: Many earlier proposals and regional flags influenced the final look. Choosing the Ashoka Chakra emphasized continuity with India’s ancient heritage and the secular, constitutional identity of modern India.

Official specifications & colors

The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) provides detailed guidelines for the exact shade, dimensions, and manufacture of the national flag. Key points:

  • Proportions: Length : Height = 3 : 2
  • Ashoka Chakra diameter is 3/4th of the height of the white band.
  • Shades: specific dye/shade references are part of manufacturing standards (to ensure consistent saffron, white and green tones).
  • Flag etiquette: it must not touch the ground, be used as a drapery, or be used for commercial purposes. Respectful disposal (by burning) is prescribed for damaged flags.

Symbolic connections

Beyond the literal color meanings, the Tiranga is associated with India’s political freedom movement, the Gandhian ideals of self-rule and simplicity (charkha era), and the constitutional values embedded after independence.

Interesting: The change from charkha to Ashoka Chakra shows a shift from a movement symbol (Gandhi’s Swadeshi) to a national emblem rooted in ancient Indian statecraft and law.

Social Reformers — their life & contribution

Short biographies and key achievements of reformers who reshaped India’s social fabric.

Mahatma Gandhi (1869–1948)

Leader of India’s independence movement, proponent of Satyagraha (non-violent resistance). Major campaigns: Non-Cooperation (1920), Civil Disobedience (1930) and Quit India (1942). Advocated communal harmony, rural uplift and simple living.

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar (1891–1956)

Principal architect of the Indian Constitution. Campaigned against caste discrimination, worked for political representation and social uplift of Dalits; promoted education, legal rights and reforms like the Hindu Code Bills.

Savitribai Phule (1831–1897)

India’s first woman teacher and pioneer of girls’ education. Along with Jyotirao Phule she opened schools for girls and lower-caste children; worked against untouchability and child marriage.

Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890)

Social activist and thinker; promoted education for marginalized communities, established schools and a home for widows, and criticized caste-based oppression and feudal structures.

Raja Ram Mohan Roy (1772–1833)

Pioneer of social and religious reform in 19th-century India — campaigned against Sati, supported widow remarriage, modern education and rationalism. Founded the Brahmo Samaj.

Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902)

Philosopher and teacher who popularized Vedanta and Yoga worldwide; inspired youth to social service, education and national pride. Instrumental in the revival of Indian spiritual thought.
